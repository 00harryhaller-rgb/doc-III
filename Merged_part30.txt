Introduction
Current Challenges in Explaining Galactic Rotation Curves
The rotation curves of galaxies—plots of orbital velocity versus distance from the galactic center—have presented one of the most persistent puzzles in modern astrophysics. Since the pioneering work of Vera Rubin in the 1970s [1], whose spectroscopic measurements first revealed the non-Keplerian rotation of Andromeda, observations have consistently shown that stars and gas in the outer regions of galaxies orbit at nearly the same velocities as those closer to the center, defying the expected Keplerian decrease in velocity with distance that would result from the visible distribution of stars and gas. This stark contradiction between measured velocity distributions and Newtonian predictions has fundamentally challenged our cosmic understanding for decades. Classical physics dictates that orbital velocity should
decrease with distance according to v(r) ∝  GM (r) , where M (r) represents enclosed mass within radius r. Reality presents a different picture: rotation curves persistently maintain flat profiles at galactic peripheries, with near-constant velocities extending to visible boundaries. Sofue and Rubin’s definitive 2001 compilation [3] systematically demonstrated this pattern across numerous spiral galaxies, while Lelli and colleagues’ Baryonic Tully-Fisher analysis (2016) [19] further validated these findings across additional galaxy types. This profound mismatch between theory and observation has led to several proposed explanations, with dark matter emerging as the dominant paradigm, as synthesized in Bertone and Hooper’s (2018) historical review [17] of dark matter research and formalized in Navarro, Frenk and White’s (1996) influential cold dark matter halo model [4]. However, despite extensive efforts spanning decades, direct detection of dark matter particles remains elusive, as evidenced by the continued development of increasingly sensitive experiments like those described by Adhikari et al. (2020) [16] and comprehensively reviewed by Tulin and Yu (2018) [30], raising questions about whether alternative explanations might better account for the observed phenomena.

Limitations of Dark Matter Models
The dark matter hypothesis proposes that galaxies are embedded within massive halos of non-baryonic matter that interacts gravitationally but does not emit, absorb, or scatter electromagnetic radiation significantly. While this hypothesis successfully explains many observed phenomena, it faces several critical limitations:

Detection Absence: Despite its widespread acceptance, the dark matter paradigm faces several critical shortcomings. Experimental efforts to detect dark matter particles di-rectly have yielded no conclusive evidence, despite increasingly sophisticated detection sys-tems like the cryogenic silicon architecture developed by Adhikari’s team (2020) [16] for the SPICE/HeRALD experiment. This persistent absence of direct detection raises fundamen-tal questions about whether dark matter represents physical reality or merely a theoretical convenience to salvage existing gravitational models
Distribution Challenges: Dark matter distribution models require increasingly com-plex fine-tuning to match observed rotation curves, as evidenced by Li and colleagues’ ex-haustive 2020 catalog [10] of halo models for SPARC galaxies. These distributions vary considerably between galaxies without clear underlying physical principles governing such variations, suggesting an ad hoc approach rather than a fundamental explanation.
Small-Scale Discrepancies: At smaller scales, standard dark matter models frequently conflict with observations—manifesting as the missing satellites problem, the core-cusp prob-lem, and other discrepancies thoroughly analyzed in Bullock and Boylan-Kolchin’s compre-hensive 2017 examination [28] of small-scale CDM challenges. These inconsistencies require additional modifications that further complicate the dark matter framework.
Philosophical Concerns: The introduction of a substance that comprises approxi-mately 85 percent of the universe’s matter content but remains undetectable except through its gravitational effects raises epistemological questions about the nature of scientific explana-tion and the criteria for accepting theoretical entities, as critically examined in Hossenfelder and McGaugh’s (2018) analysis [18] of dark matter alternatives.
These limitations have motivated the search for alternative explanations that might account for galactic rotation curves without requiring additional mass beyond what is directly ob-servable, including Verlinde’s (2017) emergent gravity theory [6], Kroupa et al.’s (2012) critique of standard cosmology [7], Milgrom’s (2015) MOND formalism [8], and Famaey and McGaugh’s (2012) review of modified gravity approaches [9].
