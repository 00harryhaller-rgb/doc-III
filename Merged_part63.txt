ABSTRACT. Contemporary cosmology faces unresolved questions regarding the nature of dark matter and dark energy. To address these challenges, this study introduces the source energy field theory (SEFT), a novel framework that reinterprets cosmic structure formation. SEFT envisions the universe as permeated by a fundamental energy field, where cosmological redshift arises not from accelerated expansion but from distance-dependent attenuation of electromagnetic energy and spatial curvature induced by the field. A general nonlinear wave equation is introduced to describe these dynamics, linking redshift to right ascension, declination, and distance.
The model was optimized using 1,701 observational data points from Pantheon+ and SH0ES, including Type Ia supernovae and Cepheid variables (6.3–17241 Mpc), and compared with the flat lambda cold dark matter ΛCDM model. SEFT achieved lower RMSE (145.521 vs. 147.665 Mpc), higher R² (0.9910 vs. 0.9908), and superior model selection criteria (Δ𝐴𝐼𝐶 = −41.753 Δ𝐵𝐼𝐶 = −19.997). These results provide strong statistical evidence for SEFT, highlighting its potential to complement or extend the ΛCDM paradigm.

INTRODUCTION
Theoretical frameworks describing the universe have historically evolved by identifying fundamental physical quantities. Newtonian mechanics, for example, was built upon the concept of absolute time, systematically describing motion and space in temporal terms. Subsequently, Einstein revolutionized physics by adopting the speed of light as a universal constant, establishing the mass–energy equivalence E
= mc², and further demonstrating that gravitational fields originate from mass and energy, positioning them as the source of spacetime curvature.
In this study, we extend Einstein’s mass–energy equivalence by proposing a novel conceptual framework in which energy is not only equivalent to mass, but is also fundamentally equivalent to distance and time. Within this paradigm, energy is positioned as the foundational entity from which mass, distance, and time emerge. This energy-centric framework offers the potential for a unified description of
cosmological phenomena.
While string theory compellingly describes matter and forces as vibrational modes of minuscule strings, it does not fully elucidate how these vibrations result in, or constrain, the fabric of space and time. To address this gap, we introduce the source energy field theory (SEFT)—a theoretical framework that characterizes all physical phenomena as nonlinear, wave-like modulations within a universal energy field. Within SEFT, cosmological redshift and other distance-dependent phenomena arise naturally from the spatial and temporal characteristics of this energy field.
SEFT is not intended to supplant existing theories, but rather to provide a complementary perspective that may inspire further advancements in cosmology. First, we outline the theoretical foundation of SEFT, and then apply its distance-specific formulation to an integrated observational dataset comprising 1,701 measurements from Pantheon+ Type Ia supernovae and SH0ES Cepheid variable stars, spanning distances

THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
Fundamental concepts and formulation

The SEFT framework aims to provide a unified description of diverse physical phenomena, including matter formation, gravitational interactions, electromagnetic phenomena, and the emergence of cosmic structures, by interpreting these phenomena as orderly modulations of the universal energy field, denoted by Ψ. In this context, "modulation" refers to spatiotemporal variations in the structural configuration and energy distribution within the electromagnetic field. This approach opens the possibility of coherently reinterpreting conventional physical concepts—such as fields, particles, gravity, electromagnetism, and the strong and weak nuclear forces—as different manifestations of these fundamental modulations.
The energy field Ψ, which may be represented as a real or complex scalar quantity, characterizes the local energy distribution at every point in spacetime. Modulations of this energy field are physically realized through wave propagation and nonlinear self-interactions. Within this theoretical framework, the concepts of “resonance” and “nonresonance” serve as central organizing principles. Resonance occurs when
observation techniques. However, this non-resonance is specific to electromagnetic waves; the same energy field may still resonate with other types of waves, such as gravitational waves. Therefore, whether a field demonstrates resonance or non-resonance is fundamentally determined by the type of wave—and thus the observational method—being employed. Within the theoretical framework of SEFT, phenomena that are invisible to electromagnetic waves—such as dark matter and dark energy—may, in principle, become observable as resonant phenomena when gravitational waves are employed as observational tools.
In this context, these phenomena are theoretically defined as follows:
Dark matter: A gravitational field that is non-resonant with electromagnetic waves but potentially resonant with gravitational waves, rendering it, in principle, detectable through gravitational wave observations.
Dark energy: An energetic field hypothesized to be largely non-resonant with both electromagnetic and gravitational waves.
Regarding mass formation, the theory suggests that resonance may facilitate the localization of energy, potentially leading to the emergence of stable, particle-like energy structures. However, this hypothesis remains speculative and requires further theoretical development and observational testing.
Therefore, SEFT provides a unified and flexible interpretation of cosmic phenomena, adaptable to specific observational contexts.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
In this study, we developed the foundational framework of the SEFT and directly derived a distance-specific redshift–distance relation from its underlying nonlinear wave equation. The proposed model explicitly accounted for electromagnetic wave attenuation, cosmic spatial curvature, and directional dependence based on right ascension and declination. This comprehensive approach enabled a more comprehensive representation of observational data compared with conventional isotropic models.
Statistical analysis using the integrated Pantheon+ and SH0ES datasets revealed that the SEFT model provided a significantly improved fit to the observations relative to the standard flat ΛCDM model. Specifically, the SEFT model achieved a lower RMSE of 145.521 Mpc (ΛCDM: 147.665 Mpc) and a higher R² of 0.991062 (ΛCDM: 0.990796). Moreover, the information criteria yielded ΔAIC = − 41.753 and ΔBIC = − 19.997 both negative, indicating “very

strong” statistical support for the SEFT model. These results substantiated the physical validity of SEFT. From a theoretical perspective, these findings indicate that the cosmological redshift may be interpreted not solely as a consequence of accelerated expansion, but rather as the combined result of distance-dependent electromagnetic	wave	attenuation	and	spatial curvature caused by a fundamental energy field. SEFT provides a unified framework in which visible matter, dark matter, and dark energy are interpreted as distinct manifestations of a single underlying energy field, whose behavior is governed by nonlinear wave equations.
Furthermore, SEFT not only achieves superior numerical agreement with observational data but also provides the flexibility to incorporate additional phenomena, such as anisotropy and inhomogeneity. This adaptability enables its application across a diverse array of cosmological datasets. Future research should extend analyses to encompass a broader redshift range and incorporate other cosmological probes, such as the cosmic microwave background (CMB) and Baryon acoustic oscillation (BAO), alongside high-resolution numerical simulations to validate the predictive accuracy of the model. Additionally, further exploration of the resonance-based mass generation mechanism intrinsic to SEFT may yield valuable insights into the relationship between particle-scale physics and the formation of large-scale cosmic structures.
Despite the statistical improvements demonstrated by SEFT, this study is associated with specific limitations. The analysis was restricted to supernovae and Cepheid datasets, and further validation using independent cosmological probes (such as CMB and BAO) is required. Recent large-scale surveys, such as extended Baryon Oscillation Spectroscopic Survey [14], Pantheon+ [15], and Dark Energy Survey [16], have provided precise cosmological constraints, and their results should be incorporated in future work to further validate SEFT. Moreover, the distance-specific formulation was derived phenomenologically and does not represent a full analytical solution of the nonlinear wave equation. These limitations highlight the need for further theoretical refinement and broader observational testing, which will be the focus of our future work.
In summary, the distance-dependent model grounded in SEFT emerges as a compelling and observationally
supported alternative to the ΛCDM model, offering both enhanced statistical fit and a novel theoretical perspective on the fundamental processes driving cosmic structure formation.

Introduction
A rapidly growing effort is being dedicated to the exploration of dark matter (DM) scenarios where the DM particle does not appear in isolation but as part of a richer dark sector, which may in particular feature new strong interactions [1–8]. Such dark sectors provide a variety of mechanisms to set the DM relic abundance [9–13] and lead to novel signatures at collider and fixed-target experiments [14–19]. Furthermore, dark sectors with significant self-interactions may explain astrophysical small scale structure observations that are in tension with predictions of collisionless cold DM simulations [20].
The phenomenology of the model depends decisively on the internal properties of the dark sector, in particular the number of dark quark flavours and their respective charge assignments. These properties determine which mesons are stable and which mesons can decay into Standard Model (SM) particles, as well as the processes that can contribute to the freeze-out of the dark sector. Based on these considerations, one obtains rather strong constraints on the structure of the dark sector if cosmological constraints are to be satisfied and the observed DM relic abundance is to be reproduced. In this paper we focus on a QCD-like SU(3) dark sector, in which the dark quarks form various bound states, in particular dark pseudoscalars π and vector mesons ρ. We identify the case of 2 dark quark flavours as particularly interesting since this guarantees that all dark pions

are stable, avoiding strong constraints from the decay of dark sector particles in the late and early Universe.1 The dark pion stability allows us to study the phenomenology of strongly interacting dark sectors at ground based experiments while simultaneously being consistent with all cosmological constraints. In our set up we find that the DM relic density is set by the annihilation process ππ → ρρ, which is kinematically forbidden in the present Universe [21].
At the same time, there is substantial freedom in the form of the portal interaction that determines how the dark sector as a whole couples to the SM. The main effect of such interactions is that they induce decays of dark ρ0 mesons into SM particles. Relatively weak interactions are sufficient for the ρ0 decays to proceed sufficiently fast to keep the dark sector in thermal equilibrium with the SM during freeze-out, while somewhat stronger interactions allow for the dark sector to be probed with direct detection experiments and at the LHC. Rho mesons that decay promptly on collider scales as well as long-lived mesons are a generic possibility of these models, leading to a wide range of novel collider signatures, such as semi-visible jets [22–25] and emerging jets [17, 26].
In the present work we therefore adopt a hybrid approach, in which the dark sector is modelled in as much detail as possible (given the inherent limitations from non-perturbative physics), while the interactions with the SM are described using a simplified model similar to the ones commonly used in the context of LHC DM searches [27, 28]. Specifically, we consider a spin-1 mediator Z′ with vector couplings to both SM and dark quarks and no other interactions. For this coupling structure direct detection constraints are known to be quite strong if the DM mass is sufficiently above the GeV scale [29, 30]. However, the typical mass scale of the dark sector is largely unconstrained and can easily be of the order of a few GeV, such that direct detection constraints are weakened. If furthermore the Z′ mediator has a mass at the TeV scale, direct detection constraints are additionally suppressed, while LHC constraints become relevant, leading to an interesting interplay between the two different search strategies.
While simplified models of strongly interacting dark sectors have been proposed previ-ously in the literature (see e.g. ref. [31]), our approach differs in that the dark sector is con-structed in a way that guarantees a consistent cosmology, independent of the details of the portal interaction. Conversely, our approach differs from most models of Strongly Interact-ing Massive Particles (SIMPs) with a Z′ or dark photon mediator in the literature [16, 32] in that we allow for a more general coupling structure, in which the interactions of the Z′ are dominated by its direct couplings to SM fermions (rather than interactions induced by mixing).
We find that large parts of the interesting parameter space are presently unconstrained by direct detection experiments and by LHC searches for di-jet resonances or missing trans-verse energy. However, the fact that a typical dark shower contains both stable and unsta-ble dark mesons means that existing LHC searches are not optimised for the characteristic signatures of strongly interacting dark sectors. General phenomenology
For the discussion above there was no need to specify the mass scale of the dark sector or the mass of the Z′ mediator. From now on we will be more specific and consider a GeV-scale dark sector that interacts with the SM via a TeV-scale Z′. As discussed in detail below, GeV-scale dark sectors provide various interesting mechanisms to produce the thermal relic density, and are less constrained by direct detection experiments than heavier DM particles. The phenomenology of dark sectors with light Z′ mediators has been explored in the literature, see e.g. [16]. Here we focus on heavy TeV-scale mediators which lead to new avenues for dark sector searches at the LHC.
Since the Z′ is heavy compared to the confinement scale of the dark sector, we can calculate its production and decay in terms of free (SM and dark) quarks – this will be the topic of section 4. At low energies and in the present Universe, however, the appropriate degrees of freedom are the pseudoscalar and vector mesons that appear in the confinement phase. For the reasons outlined above, all dark pions are exactly stable in the model we

consider and can therefore potentially account for the DM in the Universe. In the present section, we will study the mechanisms that determine the relic abundance of dark pions, as well as constraints from direct detection experiments. Before doing so, we however need to consider the properties of the rho mesons, which turn out to be of central importance for the phenomenology of the model.

Astrophysical constraints
Let us finally consider potential constraints from astrophysical observations. Indirect de-tection experiments typically give strong constraints on models of thermal DM with a particle mass below 10 GeV. In our model, however, the dominant annihilation channel for dark pions is ππ → ρρ. Since mρ > mπ, this channel is only kinematically open in the early Universe and becomes strongly suppressed for small relative velocities. Thus, there are no relevant indirect detection constraints for our model.
One of the attractive features of strongly interacting dark sectors is their potential to resolve the so-called small-scale crisis of ΛCDM with DM self-interactions. In our model, the self-scattering cross section for π+π− → π+π− divided by the dark pion mass is given by
