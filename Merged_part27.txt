Introduction
General Relativity (GR) describes gravity through the curvature of spacetime and has been confirmed by precision tests ranging from solar-system dynamics to gravitational-wave propagation. However, GR treats the geometric structure of spacetime as fundamental and does not explain why gravity exists as curvature rather than as a phenomenon emerging from deeper degrees of freedom. At cosmological scales, the ΛCDM model successfully fits a wide range of observations, but it requires cold dark matter and dark energy (often modeled as a cosmological constant) whose physical origins remain unknown.
The Ensor Gravity Source Postulation (EGSP) addresses these issues by promoting dimensionality itself to a dynamical variable. The central claim is that the observed three-dimensional universe is not the ground state of spacetime. Instead, it is a metastable phase maintained by a nonzero dimensional order parameter. Perturbations away from this metastable configuration induce a relaxation process toward a lower-dimensional equilibrium. The macroscopic manifestation of this relaxation is observed as gravitational attraction.
In this framework, gravity is not a fundamental force but an emergent phenomenon arising from dimensional relaxation dynamics. The same mechanism provides a natural origin for the vacuum energy driving cosmic acceleration and supplies a candidate explanation for dark matter in the form of topological relics produced during the dimensional phase transition. Furthermore, the dynamics of this transition offer a microphysical origin for primordial magnetic fields and a novel pathway to resolve the persistent Hubble Tension.
EGSP is presented as an effective, phenomenological, and test-driven framework for exploring the emergence of gravitational dynamics from deeper degrees of freedom associated with dimensional relaxation. It is not proposed as a complete theory of quantum gravity, nor as a final ultraviolet description of spacetime. Instead, EGSP operates at the level of a low-energy effective field theory, motivated by known scalar-tensor constructions and by moduli dynamics in higher-dimensional theories, and is designed to capture observable consequences of dimensional metastability in a mathematically controlled and falsifiable manner. Its primary aim is to unify gravitational phenomenology, cosmic acceleration, and large-scale structure anomalies within a single consistent model. Furthermore, the framework provides a candidate mechanism for the origin of cosmic magnetic fields and a novel approach to resolving the Hubble Tension, positioning it at the forefront of contemporary cosmological debates. It provides clear empirical predictions - particularly in void dynamics, gravitational-wave backgrounds, and cosmic microwave background signatures - that allow the framework to be decisively tested and potentially ruled out by upcoming observations.

Conceptual Framework
Throughout this work, ‘dimensionality’ refers to the effective number of extended spacetime directions participating in low-energy dynamics, not to the topological dimension of the manifold.
2.1-Dimensional Order Parameter
The central object in EGSP is a scalar field phi interpreted as a dimensional order parameter. The value phi = 0 corresponds to the true vacuum of the theory, representing the lowest-energy dimensional configuration. The value phi = phi_3 > 0 corresponds to a metastable three-dimensional phase identified with the observed universe.
The dynamics of phi are governed by a potential V(phi) chosen to admit both a global minimum at phi = 0 and a local metastable minimum at phi = phi_3. A representative form is:
V(phi) = Lambda^4 (1 − exp(−alpha phi^2 / M_Pl^2))^2 + beta (phi / M_Pl)^3 exp(−gamma phi^2 / M_Pl^2)
where Lambda sets the characteristic energy scale of the transition, M_Pl is the reduced Planck mass, and alpha, beta, gamma are dimensionless parameters controlling the stability and shape of the metastable vacuum.
The existence of these minima allows the universe to reside for cosmological times in the metastable phase while retaining a well-defined lower-dimensional ground state.

2.2 Field-Dependent Gravitational Coupling
Gravitational dynamics in EGSP are controlled by a non-minimal coupling function f(phi) between the scalar field and curvature. The coupling function is defined as:
f(phi) = 1 − exp(−kappa phi^2 / M_Pl^2) The effective Newton constant becomes:
G_eff(phi) = G / f(phi)
In the metastable vacuum, f(phi_3) ≈ 1 and standard GR is recovered. Deviations from this value occur when phi is perturbed away from equilibrium, leading to environment-dependent gravitational behavior without introducing additional independent long-range forces. Since φ is dynamically driven to φ₃ in regions with ρ » ρ̄, all deviations are exponentially suppressed on Solar System scales.
2.3 Gravity as Dimensional Relaxation
The physical interpretation of EGSP is that gravitational phenomena arise from relaxation of the dimensional order parameter back toward its metastable configuration. Matter and energy act as sources of perturbations in phi, and the system responds by evolving

toward a configuration of reduced dimensional strain. The trajectories of matter in spacetime follow from this relaxation process, reproducing gravitational attraction while providing an underlying mechanism for its origin.
