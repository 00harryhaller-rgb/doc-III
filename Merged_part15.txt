converges better since its shape increases or decreases as H0 decreases or increases respectively. In other words, the dynamical shape of any cosmological curve depends on the value given to H0. As H0 is somehow known, curves behave better at higher redshift, alleviating convergence problems as expected. These arguments represent a further tool in order to fix model independent constraints on f (R(z)) and its derivatives. Indeed, H0 is fixed regardless the cosmological distance taken into account, by means of Eq. (5). It is possible to fix H0 in the range z < 0.4 with supernovae only. We find
H0 = 69.785+1.060 .	(6)
In cosmography, the strategy of fixing H0 in a smaller interval of data overcomes several problems associated to the well consolidated usage of auxiliary variables. Indeed, as above mentioned, the method of adopting auxiliary variables consists in determining parametric functions y(z) in terms of the redshift z, whose values rely in the interval y(z) ∈ [0, 1], as z → 0 and z → ∞ respectively. This procedure rearranges catalog data and suffers from severe shortcomings [33, 34]. Indeed, the form of y(z) is not known a priori and any possible reparameterized variable should guarantee that errors do not deeply propagate in the statistical analysis. In several cases, y(z) variables are therefore inconsistent with low redshift cosmography, providing misleading results, albeit their use becomes more relevant for high redshift data sets.
In our case, we propose to fix H0 as a low redshift cosmographic setting value, since all distances reduce to Eq. (5) at a first order of Taylor expansions. Our corresponding best fit intervals are compatible with previous analysis [35] and guarantee that errors do not significatively propagate on measured coefficients. For our purposes, the strategy of fixing H0 by means of small redshift data only better behaves than standard auxiliary variables, due to the fact that z ≤ 1 (see for recent applications [36–38]), although it would fail at higher redshift domains. Since, in our cases, z reaches the upper value z ∼ 1.414, i.e. the maximum z of the supernova compilation, we expect that the use of Eq.
(5) to get H0 would guarantee refined best fit results with respect to any possible reparameterized auxiliary variables.
An additional technique is to combine more than one data set to infer cosmological bounds. Indeed, although we treat bad convergence of truncated series by using Eq. (6), further data sets would improve the quality of numerical estimates. Hence, the CS and the derived constraints on f (R) derivatives would improve consequently. Here, we combine supernovae data with the baryonic acoustic oscillation measurement. It is possible to show that such a choice actually reduces the convergence problem.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS

In this paper, we present a method to fix constraints on curvature dark energy in the context of f (R) gravity by using a cosmographic analysis derived from different cosmological distances. Cosmography is here adopted to determine model independent bounds on observables related to f (R) and its derivatives. We follow this procedure by keeping in mind that each term of CS has a precise physical meaning, and allows to infer dynamical properties of f (R) curvature fluid. Rephrasing this point, the limits on CS allow to recover present time bounds on the dark energy dynamics, regardless the particular cosmological model. In so doing, to alleviate the duality problem among cosmological distances, we investigate cosmography by means of the luminosity, flux and angular distances. The approach consists in using cosmography to fix cosmological bounds on curvature fluid and to choose a combination of causal distances in order to alleviate degeneracy among cosmological models. Determining cosmographic results through the three mentioned distances leads to tighter intervals of measurements, showing that values of curvature dark energy pressure rely in lower bounds than the intervals expected in a pure ΛCDM model. To this end, we used

9

two data sets, i.e. the Union 2.1 compilation of supernovae and the baryonic acoustic oscillation measurements. In our analysis, we considered the today observed value of the Hubble parameter H0 as a prior. This choice is due to the need of alleviating the convergence problem which plagues cosmography as soon as cosmological data exceed the range z > 1. Thus, we fix H0 as a sort of initial condition on the three distances, by means of supernovae only, using
1
all the distances expanded at the lowest order of z, that is dL;F ;A ∼	z. The corresponding fitting value of H0
0
has been obtained in the supernovae range z < 0.4 where the convergence problem does not exist. This technique is
developed in order to determine better the values of q0, j0 and s0, without introducing any re-parametrization variable of the redshift. In addition, we get constraints on f (z) derivatives up to the second order in the Taylor expansion around z = 0 and found that the acceleration parameter q0 is almost compatible when inferred from dL while the jerk and snap parameters are only compatible with j0 ≥ 1, s0 > 1 respectively. These outcomes indicate again that the standard cosmological paradigm, namely the ΛCDM model, may be extended in terms of curvature dark energy fluid. Finally, we proposed, as test function, a particular f (z), able to reproduce the cosmographic bounds that fairly well fits the Hubble diagram up to fourth order expansion in powers of a(t). In future research, we will refine this approach by combining more cosmological tests and further distances. This approach will allow to reduce the phase spaces and better constrain the free parameters of curvature dark energy.

Abstract

Realizing dark energy and the observed de Sitter spacetime in quantum gravity has proven to be obstructed in most every usual approach. We ar-gue that additional degrees of freedom of the left- and right-movers in string theory and a resulting doubled, non-commutatively generalized geometric formulation thereof can lead to an effective model of dark energy consistent with de Sitter spacetime. In this approach, the curvature of the canoni-cally conjugate dual space provides for the dark energy inducing a positive cosmological constant in the observed spacetime, whereas the size of the above dual space is the gravitational constant in the same observed de Sit-ter spacetime. As a hallmark relation owing to a unique feature of string theory which relates short distances to long distances, the cosmological con-stant scale, the Planck scale, and the effective TeV-sized particle physics scale must satisfy a see-saw-like formula—precisely the generic prediction of certain stringy cosmic brane type models.

Whether or not asymptotically de Sitter space can be found in a consistent theory of quantum gravity and matter including string theory [1] has captured much attention ever since the dramatic discovery of dark energy in the late 1990s [2, 3]. (For the most recent measurements of the Hubble constant and the associated discrepancies, see [4–6]; see below.) The existence and realization of de Sitter space as a solution in string theory is still considered and open question [7], and the interest in this fundamental issue has been recently reignited in [8, 9]; see also [10, 11]. We argue that one can successfully address the problem of dark energy and the observed de Sitter spacetime in a generic, non-commutative generalized geometric phase-space formulation of string theory. Essentially, the curvature and size of the canonically conjugate dual space are the cosmological and gravitational constants in the observed spacetime, respectively. Furthermore, the three scales associated with: (1) the cosmological constant, (2) the Planck units and (3) the effective particle physics, are related by a see-saw-like formula via T-duality. This is a hallmark feature of string theory that relates reciprocally short and long distances, and precisely the generic prediction of certain toy models [12].

Context and Position Within the Tier–0 Framework
This paper occupies a law-level admissibility clarification role within the Tier–0 framework. Its purpose is not to propose a new dark energy field, introduce a modified gravitational law, or reinterpret cosmological data through model-dependent dynamics. Rather, it identifies a structural reason why the background component inferred from late-time cosmological expansion has resisted material identification, and it establishes the admissible law-level class that such a component must occupy once closure, persistence, and boundary canonicity are enforced.
The Tier–0 framework was introduced in The Everything Equation: Universal Law Structure from Boundary Involution, Collapse, and Ω–Closure (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18081205), where admissibility of a candidate law L was defined by the fixed-point condition

L = Ω ∆ ∂(L).

That work establishes a domain-independent criterion for lawful structure, separating questions of ontology, microphysical realization, or phenomenological modeling from structural necessity.
A physical instantiation of the Tier–0 framework appears in The Everything Equation in Physics: A Closure Principle for Physical Lawhood (Zenodo DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.18080442), which argues that boundary restriction, dissipative persistence, and reflective closure already organize standard physical theory. In that setting, admissible structures are those whose predictions remain invariant under boundary-limited perturbations and collapse-filtered persistence, independent of interpretive overlay.
The present paper applies this admissibility criterion to a longstanding and well-documented cosmological requirement: the presence of a persistent, approximately homogeneous contribution to the late-time curvature and expansion budget beyond that supplied by baryonic matter and radiation. Conventional approaches encode this requirement as a new energy component or effective fluid, often interpreted as vacuum energy or a dynamical field evolving within the same record-bearing sector as ordinary matter.

Rather than proposing a new dynamical model or altering gravitational dynamics, this paper demonstrates that the difficulty is structural. A global curvature contribution need not correspond to a Λ-sector, record-bearing substance to be admissible. Boundary normalization removes the assumption that all curvature sources must be material entities; collapse filters out structures that would necessarily generate dissipative or record-bearing signatures; and reflective closure admits stable background contributions that persist under cosmological coarse-graining while remaining silent in ordinary detection channels.
As a consequence, the empirically required dark energy component is most coherently classified as an Ω-dominant closure background: a gravitationally active, non-localized contribution that is stable under admissible re-expression and ∆-silent with respect to dissipation, thermalization, and record formation. The absence of direct non-gravitational detection is thereby reinterpreted not as a failure of experimental sensitivity, but as a consequence of sector misclassification at the level of lawhood.
Accordingly, this paper should be read as a structural classification rather than a competing cosmological model. It preserves the empirical content of standard dark energy phenomenology while explaining why material interpretations and dynamical microphysical proposals remain under-determined before admissibility is even considered. Acceptance of the broader Tier–0 framework is not required to follow the argument, but it provides a unifying context in which the global expansion evidence and the systematic absence of record-bearing signatures become mutually consistent and independently intelligible.

Abstract
Dark energy is inferred observationally as a persistent, approximately homogeneous con-tribution to the late-time expansion of the Universe, yet it remains undetected in ordinary matter-sector channels. Standard treatments encode this component as an effective energy density or fluid, often interpreted as vacuum energy or a new dynamical field. In this work we revisit the problem at the level of lawhood rather than microphysical modeling, using the Tier–0 admissibility framework as a structural diagnostic.
We argue that the defining empirical profile of dark energy—global curvature influence, absence of clustering, and systematic silence with respect to dissipative record formation—is inconsistent with a conventional Λ-sector (record-bearing) substance. Instead, we classify dark energy as an Ω-dominant, ∆-silent closure background: a closure-stable contribution to the gravitational sector that persists under cosmological coarse-graining while remaining outside ordinary record-bearing channels.
This classification preserves standard gravitational dynamics and the effective utility of ΛCDM parameterizations, while clarifying why non-gravitational detection is not generically expected and why the observed equation-of-state is close to w ≃ −1. The result is not a new dynamical model but a law-level reorganization: dark energy is identified as a background curvature term fixed by admissibility and closure, rather than as a hidden material sector. The framework provides a minimal and conservative interpretation of cosmic acceleration and sharpens the space of viable microphysical realizations without modifying established physics.

Introduction
The empirical case for a late-time acceleration of the cosmic expansion is now supported by multiple observational channels. In the standard cosmological description, these data are summarized by an additional component commonly called dark energy: an effectively homogeneous contribution to the large-scale curvature budget that dominates the present-day expansion history while remaining invisible to ordinary matter-sector detectors. Unlike dark matter, whose primary signature is clustering and halo formation, the defining feature of dark energy is global: it alters the background evolution without participating in local dissipative dynamics.
Despite the practical success of the ΛCDM parameterization, the ontology of the dark energy component remains conceptually unstable. The dominant phenomenological fit corresponds to an equation-of-state parameter near w ≃ −1, consistent with a cosmological constant term, yet standard field-theoretic interpretations of “vacuum energy” notoriously encounter severe magnitude and naturalness tensions. Alternative proposals introduce new scalar degrees of freedom, modified gravity actions, or dynamical fluids, but these often shift the difficulty rather than resolve it, and they substantially expand the space of model-dependent assumptions.
This motivates a structural question logically prior to microphysical modeling: what kind of entity or structure can play the dark energy role under a law-level criterion of admissibility? The observational evidence constrains a gravitational effect a persistent, approximately homogeneous curvature contribution at cosmological scales but it does not by itself determine whether that

contribution corresponds to a record-bearing, dissipative matter sector, to a new dynamical field, or to a distinct admissible class of structure.
The present paper proposes that the dark energy problem has been approached primarily through naive object-based language. Dark energy is tacitly treated as a “substance” with an energy density, or as a dynamical field evolving in time, and one then seeks a microphysical mechanism that produces the observed expansion history. In contrast, the defining observational profile of dark energy is characterized by the absence of the signatures typically associated with ordinary matter sectors: it does not radiate, does not thermalize, does not cluster into halos, and does not produce detectable non-gravitational records. These absences are not merely unknowns; they are diagnostic constraints on admissible structure.
Our objective is therefore classificatory. Working within the Tier–0 admissibility framework, we develop a law-level characterization of dark energy as a global closure background: a curvature contribution that is stable under reflective closure and remains silent with respect to collapse-mediated record formation. The central claim is not that one must add new dynamics, modify general relativity, or select a particular scalar-field model. Rather, we argue that the observed dark energy role is most coherently understood as an Ω-dominant closure background: a contribution to the gravitational sector that is invariant under admissible re-expression and does not enter the dissipative record-bearing channel that governs ordinary matter.
This perspective preserves all standard physics anchors. No modification of Einstein gravity is assumed, no new long-range forces are postulated, and the ΛCDM phenomenology is treated as an effective parameterization rather than as an ontological commitment. The proposal is that the correct resolution lies at the level of admissibility classification: the data constrain which sector of lawhood the dark energy component inhabits, not merely which microphysical mechanism one prefers.
Scope and contribution. The contribution of this paper is threefold:
We formulate a law-level criterion distinguishing dissipative, record-bearing matter sectors from global curvature contributions that remain observationally silent except through the background expansion.
We show that the defining phenomenology of dark energy homogeneity, non-clustering, and the absence of non-gravitational records is structurally consistent with an Ω-dominant, ∆-silent closure background.
We clarify the distinction between this classification and modified gravity or dynamical dark energy models: the present work does not alter gravitational dynamics, but classifies the admissible status of a global curvature component within a closure framework.

Non-claims. To avoid confusion, we emphasize what this paper does not claim. We do not propose a new dark energy particle, scalar field, or dynamical fluid, nor do we modify the gravitational field equations. We do not claim to solve the cosmological constant numerical hierarchy by introducing

a cancellation mechanism. We also do not attempt a unified dark-sector model in this work. The paper is strictly concerned with law-level structure: what is required for a component to supply a persistent, homogeneous curvature role while remaining silent with respect to ∆-mediated record formation.

Phenomenology of dark energy as a structural profile
This section summarizes the empirical “dark energy” evidence in a form suited to Tier–0 classification. The purpose is not to review observational cosmology in detail, but to isolate the structural content of the data: what the dark energy component demonstrably does and does not do. These features will later be matched to admissibility sectors under the Tier–0 operators.

What the dark energy component demonstrably does
At the level of large-scale phenomenology, dark energy is inferred as a contribution that alters the background expansion history of the Universe at late times. The common observational content can be stated without committing to a microphysical model:
It contributes to the global curvature/expansion budget. The data require an additional component beyond baryons and radiation to account for the observed late-time expansion behavior. In standard parameterizations this appears as an approximately constant energy density term or an effective fluid with negative pressure.
It dominates at late times. The inferred component is subdominant during radiation and much of matter domination but becomes dynamically significant in the recent cosmological epoch.
It is approximately homogeneous on large scales. Unlike dark matter, which clusters and forms halos, the dark energy component does not appear as a localized mass distribution. Its primary signature is background-level rather than halo-level.

Its effective equation-of-state is near w ≃ −1. Current observational constraints are consistent with an equation-of-state close to that of a cosmological constant, with only limited room for redshift evolution.
These points constitute the minimal gravitational signature: dark energy is operationally “whatever supplies the required late-time background curvature contribution”.

What the dark energy component demonstrably does not do
Equally important are the systematic absences. Dark energy is characterized by the lack of signatures typical of ordinary matter sectors:
No electromagnetic emission, absorption, or scattering attributable to dark energy. There is no evidence for radiation channels, spectral features, or thermodynamic footprints tied to the dark energy component.
No robust evidence of thermalization or dissipative dynamics. Dark energy does not exchange heat with baryonic matter in any observed manner, and it does not appear to participate in irreversible relaxation processes analogous to those governing ordinary matter.
No clustering into halos or bound structures. In contrast to dark matter, the dark energy component does not form halo-like distributions. Its influence is not detected through localized gravitational potentials but through the global expansion history.
No conclusive non-gravitational detection. Dark energy has not been detected by direct laboratory measurement as a matter-sector field or particle. Its empirical presence is inferred through gravitational and geometric observables.
These absences are not merely gaps in knowledge. They are structural constraints: the component required by the data is silent with respect to the usual ∆-mediated record channels that render ordinary sectors visible and dissipative.

The resulting structural tension
Combining the two lists yields the core tension:

Dark energy contributes to the global curvature/expansion budget while remaining ap-proximately homogeneous, non-clustering, and silent in ordinary record-bearing channels.

Dark energy as an Ω-dominant closure background

Definition of the dark energy class

In Tier–0 terms, dark energy occupies a channel that is not selected into the record-bearing Λ-sector by ∆, and whose defining feature is closure stability at the background level rather than local material evolution.

Immediate consequences
The classification in Definition 5.1 yields several immediate structural consequences, without introducing new dynamics.

Why non-gravitational detection is not expected.  Detectors operate by producing
∆-stabilized records. If the dominant dark energy component is ∆-silent and background-like, then the absence of direct detection is not surprising and does not require extreme coupling suppression. The correct expectation is that dark energy is inferred through geometric/gravitational observables rather than through localized interaction events.

Why homogeneity and non-clustering are natural. A Λ-sector substance typically admits localized excitations, clustering, and dissipative morphology. By contrast, an Ω-dominant closure background is defined precisely by its persistence under coarse-graining and by its invariance under admissible re-expression; therefore it appears as a background contribution rather than as a halo-forming component.

Why w ≃ −1 is structurally favored. Thus, the observed proximity to w ≃ −1 is consistent with the closure-background classification.

Why microphysical identity is underdetermined. The classification fixes the admis-sibility role but does not, by itself, choose a microscopic model. Any candidate realization must reproduce the background, ∆-silent, non-clustering profile. This immediately disfavors broad classes of proposals that would imply dissipation, detectable couplings, or localized structure formation.

Compatibility with standard gravitational dynamics
Nothing in Definition 5.1 requires modification of Einstein gravity. The point is instead that the effective source of the gravitational field need not be exhausted by ordinary Λ-sector matter content,

and that admissibility allows an Ω-stable background contribution that remains record-silent. In GR language, one may regard dark energy as an effective background contribution T DE whose microphysical interpretation is not fixed, but whose Tier–0 sector classification is constrained: it is global, ∆-silent, and Ω-stable.
This stance differs from modified gravity approaches. Why dark energy is not Λ-sector matter
This section makes explicit the structural incompatibility between the observed dark energy profile and a conventional Λ-sector (record-bearing, dissipative) substance. The argument is not that a hidden substance is impossible, but that treating dark energy as a material sector is structurally disfavored under the Tier–0 diagnostics: it introduces properties that would generically produce
∆-mediated records, localization, or dissipative signatures that are not observed.

Λ-sector structure implies record-bearing signatures
By definition, a Λ-sector component is ∆-active: it participates in collapse-mediated persistence, generates or stabilizes records, and admits operational time ordering via record update. In physical realizations this is expressed through one or more of the following generic signatures:
coupling to detectors and the formation of localized records,
thermodynamic exchange (heat flow, equilibration),
dissipative relaxation, mixing, or decoherence in matter-sector channels,
localization and clustering morphology under gravitational instability,

radiative or scattering footprints when coupled to electromagnetism.

These are not optional stylistic features; they are what it means for a sector to be ∆-active in practice. Consequently, interpreting dark energy as a hidden Λ-sector substance entails the expectation of some corresponding record-bearing footprint, unless one introduces an additional layer of screening, tuning, or symmetry-based decoupling to suppress all such channels.

Dark energy exhibits systematic ∆-silence
The defining observational profile of dark energy is a persistent background influence with:

no electromagnetic visibility,

no thermalization or dissipative exchange with baryons,

no halo formation or localized clustering,

no laboratory-scale record-bearing detection channel.

Taken individually, each absence might be attributed to weak coupling. Taken together and persisting across environments, they constitute a structural diagnostic: the component required by cosmology behaves as ∆-silent rather than ∆-active.
This is the key point. Weak coupling is a contingent microphysical parameter; ∆-silence is a sector classification. Under Tier–0, the latter provides the cleaner explanation: the dark energy component does not reside in the record-bearing Λ-sector and therefore does not generically produce record-bearing signatures.

Why localization and clustering are decisive
The strongest structural distinction between dark energy and a conventional substance is non-localization. A Λ-sector substance typically admits localized excitations and, under gravity, tends to cluster unless prevented by large pressure, strong interactions, or fine-tuned dynamics. Dark energy, by contrast, appears approximately homogeneous and does not form halos. This feature is not an afterthought; it is central to the inference of dark energy itself.
From the Tier–0 perspective, non-localization is a boundary/closure signature: background terms that persist under cosmological coarse-graining are naturally interpreted as closure-stable law content rather than as an additional local material sector. In other words, the globality of dark energy points toward an Ω-dominant closure background rather than a Λ-sector substance.

Vacuum energy language as a representational category error
A common category error is to treat the ΛCDM background term as literally the energy density of vacuum degrees of freedom. While quantum field theory admits vacuum contributions in formal

expressions, interpreting the observed background term as a material vacuum substance imports an ontological commitment that the data do not supply and that Tier–0 does not require.
In the present framework, the cosmological constant term (or its effective equivalent) is more coherently interpreted as a closure-stable background contribution: a piece of law content fixed by boundary normalization and reflective completion at the cosmological scale. Under this reading, the “vacuum energy problem” is reclassified as a mismatch between ontology language and admissibility structure: the background term is not an energy reservoir of a material sector, but an Ω-stable closure contribution to the gravitational sector.

Global curvature, expansion, and the role of time

Background terms as closure-stable law content

Expansion as a record-bearing phenomenon and the status of dark energy

Dark energy is therefore not “experienced” directly; it is inferred through the way it shapes the record-bearing sector’s global geometry.
This perspective resolves a common conceptual tension. If dark energy is treated as a substance “pushing” the Universe apart, one is tempted to ascribe local dynamical agency to it. In the closure-background classification, dark energy is instead a global curvature term that constrains the background geometry within which record-bearing processes occur. Why w ≃ −1 fits the closure-background role
The empirical preference for an equation-of-state near w ≃ −1 is consistent with a term that is approximately constant under the relevant coarse-graining and does not introduce additional internal degrees of freedom. Thus, the closure-background classification provides a structural rationale for why the simplest fit (w ≃ −1) is favored: an Ω-stable background term is expected to be close to constant over the epoch in which it dominates, because it is defined by closure persistence rather than by a local material evolution law.

Distinction from dark matter in law-level terms

Discussion
The aim of this paper has been to make a law-level determination about the dark energy component: whether the empirically required background term should be treated as a hidden Λ-sector substance or as a distinct admissibility class of structure. The Tier–0 perspective motivates the second classification. In this section we summarize the conceptual payoff, clarify what is newly explained, and outline the minimal set of questions that remain open.

What is gained by the Ω-dominant classification
The principal gain is a coherent resolution of a tension that otherwise persists across models:
Dark energy contributes to the global curvature/expansion budget while remaining ap-proximately homogeneous, non-clustering, and silent in ordinary record-bearing channels.
Within object-based language this appears as a mysterious “substance” that drives acceleration without leaving any local footprint, motivating either increasingly elaborate microphysics or modified gravity. In Tier–0 terms the situation is cleaner: the absence of localized records is not a mystery if the dominant component is not in the record-bearing Λ-sector. The Ω-dominant closure-background classification explains in a unified way why:
the primary evidence for dark energy is geometric and gravitational (background expansion),
non-gravitational detection is not expected as a generic matter-sector signature,
homogeneity and non-clustering are natural rather than anomalous, and
the empirical preference for w ≃ −1 is consistent with closure stability under coarse-graining.
This is not a replacement of cosmological phenomenology; it is an admissibility interpretation of why the phenomenology has the structure it does.

Why “vacuum energy” remains underdetermined by observation
Cosmological inference constrains a background curvature contribution. It does not specify a microscopic energy reservoir. Treating the inferred term as the literal energy density of vacuum degrees of freedom therefore imports an ontological commitment not fixed by the data. The Tier–0 classification reframes the situation: the observed background term is best understood as a piece of closure-stable law content, not as an ordinary substance. On this view, the “vacuum energy problem” is partly a category error: it arises from insisting on a Λ-sector interpretation of a term whose phenomenology is ∆-silent and global.
This does not claim to resolve the numerical hierarchy associated with naive field-theoretic vacuum estimates; rather, it clarifies why the observed term need not be interpreted as a material vacuum energy at all.

Relation to the broader Tier–0 programme
The classification developed here is not isolated. It sits naturally within the Tier–0 organization of lawhood into boundary normalization, collapse persistence, and reflective closure. Dark energy occupies a maximally global role: it is a curvature contribution that persists as background law content under cosmological coarse-graining, stable under admissible re-expression, and silent in record-bearing channels. This positioning complements the Tier–0 classifications of null structure and of Λ-sector dissipation without requiring a unified dark-sector ontology.

What remains open
The present work is classificatory and therefore leaves several questions open, now in a more precise form.

Realization mechanisms. If one insists on a microphysical realization of the background term, what mechanism produces an effectively Ω-stable, ∆-silent, non-clustering contribution at late times without introducing new record-bearing signatures? Many model classes can be evaluated against this structural constraint.

Empirical discriminators. What observations would discriminate an Ω-dominant closure background from dynamical dark energy models with mild evolution of w(z)? The present paper does not fit data or propose new parameters, but the classification suggests where discriminators would live: departures from homogeneity, clustering behavior, or evidence of matter-sector couplings would weaken the closure-background assignment.

Relation to dark matter and future synthesis. This paper has deliberately avoided a unified dark-sector claim. Nevertheless, the Tier–0 approach suggests a structural unification at the level of admissibility: both dark matter and dark energy are curvature contributions that are largely silent in ordinary record-bearing channels, yet they differ in localization and globality. A separate synthesis is possible once the individual classifications are established.

The Tier–0 admissibility framework provides a minimal and conservative interpretation of dark energy: it is best classified not as an additional Λ-sector substance but as an Ω-dominant, ∆-silent closure background. Interpretation note
The classification is compatible with effective parameterizations (e.g. ΛCDM, w(z) fluids) and with microphysical models (e.g. scalar fields) but does not require them. Any model is acceptable insofar as it realizes the Ω-dominant closure-background role while remaining effectively ∆-silent and non-clustering in the regimes constrained by observation.

Falsifiability and weakening conditions
This appendix states explicit conditions under which the Ω-dominant closure-background classifica-tion would be weakened or would require revision. The purpose is not to forecast future data, but to make clear that the thesis is a structural, testable sector assignment rather than an unfalsifiable interpretive overlay.

What would weaken the ∆-silence assignment
The central diagnostic in the main text is ∆-silence: the absence of generic record-bearing signatures attributable to the dominant dark energy component. The classification would be weakened if robust evidence emerged for any of the following:
Direct non-gravitational detection. A reproducible laboratory detection of a dark energy field or particle that produces localized, ∆-stabilized records in ordinary detectors at a level

that cannot be interpreted as a subdominant effect would contradict the claim that the dominant component is record-silent.
Electromagnetic or thermodynamic footprints. Unambiguous radiation, absorption, scattering, or thermal exchange channels correlated with the inferred dark energy background would indicate ∆-activity inconsistent with the closure-background role.
Environmental coupling signatures. Strong, reproducible dependence of the inferred dark energy component on local matter environments (beyond what is expected from standard cosmological backreaction or parameter degeneracies) could indicate a matter-sector coupling incompatible with ∆-silence.
These conditions are stated conservatively: the thesis concerns the dominant component. Subdominant couplings or effective parameter degeneracies would not by themselves refute the classification.

What would weaken the non-clustering (background) assignment
A defining feature of the Ω-dominant closure background is non-localization under the relevant coarse-graining. The classification would be weakened if robust evidence emerged that dark energy:
Clusters or localizes on sub-horizon scales in a way not attributable to ordinary matter inhomogeneities or to standard perturbative effects; or
Forms bound structures or halo-like distributions that track the dark energy component rather than the dark matter or baryon distributions.
Such behavior would suggest that the dominant component carries additional degrees of freedom with local dynamics, pushing it toward a dynamical sector rather than a closure-background role.

What would favor dynamical dark energy models over a closure background
The classification developed in the main text is consistent with mild time variation, but it is structurally aligned with the empirical preference for a nearly constant background term. The closure-background thesis would be weakened if future data established:
A statistically robust, nontrivial evolution of w(z) incompatible with a closure-stable term under reasonable coarse-graining; or
Additional degrees of freedom required by observation (e.g. scale-dependent clustering signatures or propagation effects) that cannot be absorbed into the background role without introducing ∆-active dynamics.
In such a scenario, the Tier–0 viewpoint would still constrain admissibility, but the dominant component might no longer be well-described as an Ω-dominant background.

What would point toward modified gravity
Finally, the present paper explicitly does not modify the gravitational field equations. The thesis would be disfavored (relative to modified gravity) if observational evidence accumulated that:
the inferred acceleration cannot be consistently captured by an effective source term without violating other gravitational consistency relations; or
the data require changes in the dynamical law for curvature rather than a background contribution on the source side.


Summary
The Ω-dominant closure-background classification is testable at the sector level. Evidence of generic
∆-activity (direct detection, thermodynamic signatures, or strong matter-sector couplings), evidence of clustering/localization of the dominant component, or robust departures from a background-like role would weaken or require revision of the thesis. Conversely, continued consistency with homogeneity, record silence, and background persistence supports the closure classification advanced in this paper.

Relation to ΛCDM parameterization (optional)
This appendix clarifies how the Ω-dominant closure-background classification relates to the standard ΛCDM practice of parameterizing dark energy as a cosmological constant term (or a near-constant effective fluid). The purpose is not to re-derive cosmological fits, but to emphasize that the present paper preserves the effective modeling utility of ΛCDM while reclassifying the ontological interpretation of the background term.

Why ΛCDM remains a valid effective description
In standard cosmology, the background expansion history is captured by a small number of parameters describing the relative contributions of radiation, baryons, cold dark matter, and an additional background term. The ΛCDM model is an especially successful effective parameterization because a constant background contribution is both simple and consistent with current constraints on the equation-of-state parameter w.
The Tier–0 classification does not challenge this effectiveness. Instead, it provides an interpreta-tion of why such a parameterization is stable: a closure-background term is expected to persist under coarse-graining and to remain invariant under admissible re-expression. Therefore, a constant or nearly constant background contribution is not merely a convenient fit; it is consistent with the structural role assigned to dark energy in the present framework.

Effective fluid language versus admissibility role
It is common to describe the dark energy term as an effective fluid with energy density and pressure satisfying p = wρ. This language is useful for computing the background and linear perturbation behavior but does not by itself fix ontology. In the closure-background classification, the effective parameters are understood as encoding the gravitational role of an Ω-stable, ∆-silent term rather than the microphysics of a material substance.
Accordingly, the present paper recommends a disciplined reading:

Treat the ΛCDM background term as an effective description of an admissible closure background, not as direct evidence for a record-bearing vacuum substance.


On the cosmological constant problem
The cosmological constant problem is often stated as a mismatch between naive quantum field theory estimates of vacuum energy and the observed background term. The present paper does not solve that numerical hierarchy by introducing a cancellation mechanism. It instead isolates a category issue: interpreting the observed background term as the literal energy density of vacuum degrees of freedom is an ontological choice, not an observational necessity.
In Tier–0 terms, the observed background term is classified as closure-stable law content. This reclassification does not remove the challenge of reconciling effective field theory bookkeeping with gravitational boundary terms, but it clarifies what is and is not demanded by observation.

Summary
The Ω-dominant closure-background classification is compatible with ΛCDM as an effective phe-nomenological parameterization. The present work preserves the practical utility of standard cosmological modeling while reclassifying the background term as an admissible closure contribution rather than as a necessarily material vacuum substance.
