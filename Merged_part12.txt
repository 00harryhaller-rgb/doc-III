CONCLUSIONS

We have considered a class of dark energy models based on scalar-tensor theories given by the action (13). In these theories, expressed in the Einstein frame, the scalar field φ is coupled to the non-relativistic matter with a constant coupling Q. The action (13) is equivalent to the Brans-Dicke theory with a field potential V , where the Brans-Dicke parameter ωBD is related to the coupling Q via the relation 3 + 2ωBD = 1/2Q2. These theories inclu√de
the f (R) gravity theories and the quintessence models as special cases where the coupling is given by Q = −1/ 6
(i.e., ωBD = 0) and Q = 0 (i.e., ωBD → ∞), respectively.
We began by studying the background cosmological dynamics in a homogeneous and isotropic setting, without specifying the field potential V (φ) but under the assumption that the slope of the potential, λ = V,φ/V , is constant. The varying λ case can also be studied by treating the fixed points as instantaneous ones. We found that for a range of values of the coupling constant Q not much smaller than unity the matter era can be realized by the solution corresponding to the point (d) in Eq. (36) subject to the condition λ/Q 1. Interestingly the presence of a non-zero coupling Q leads to a de-Sitter solution characterized by the condition V,φ + QFR = 0 (i.e., λ = 4Q), which can lead to late-time acceleration. (The condition for the stability of this de-Sitter solution is given by dλ/dφ < 0 at the fixed point.)
In the absence of the scalar-field potential, solar-system tests constrain the coupling Q to have values in the range Q < 2.5  10−3. The presence of the potential, on the other hand, allows the LGC to be satisfied for larger values of Q , if the field is sufficiently heavy in the high-curvature region where gravity experiments are carried out. We found that even when Q is of the order of 1, a thin-shell can form inside a spherically symmetric body such that the effective coupling Qeff defined in Eq. (55) becomes much smaller than 1.
We then considered a family of models given by the scalar-field potentials (41) which generalize the corresponding potential in the f (R) theory, while at the same time satisfying the LGC for appropriate choices of the parameters.

Cosmic Broth: A Structurally Constrained Framework for Emergent Inertia and Cosmological Substrate Dynamics

Abstract
The origin of inertia remains one of the deepest unresolved questions in fundamental physics. While General Relativity successfully describes gravitational dynamics through spacetime geometry, inertial mass itself is introduced as a primitive property rather than a derived phenomenon. In this work we develop a structurally constrained formulation of the Cosmic Broth framework in which inertia emerges dynamically from interaction between matter and a universal correlated substrate.
We demonstrate that causality and cosmological horizon structure impose intrinsic limits on substrate correlations, fixing the relaxation scale of the medium independently of phenomenological assumptions. Integrating out substrate degrees of freedom yields a nonlocal inertial response characterized by finite memory time, leading naturally to frequency-dependent inertia in laboratory systems. Remarkably, the same causal constraint determines a characteristic acceleration scale
a⋆∼cHO,a_\star \sim c H_O,a⋆∼cHO,
linking galactic dynamics, inertial response, and cosmological expansion within a single dynamical structure.
In this framework inertia becomes a locally measured manifestation of globally constrained substrate dynamics. The equivalence principle arises as an infrared fixed point of matter–substrate interaction, while effective dark-sector behavior corresponds to equilibrium stress–energy of the substrate. We formulate a fundamental substrate relation connecting correlation length, relaxation time, and emergent inertial scale, thereby reducing previously independent phenomenological parameters to consequences of cosmological causality.
The resulting theory provides a unified description in which gravitational curvature, inertial mass, and galactic acceleration scales originate from different dynamical regimes of a single underlying medium. Precision metrology and astrophysical observations probe complementary limits of the same substrate physics, rendering emergent inertia experimentally testable across vastly different scales.

Introduction
Inertia occupies a peculiar position in modern physics. Unlike gravitational interaction, which in General Relativity arises from spacetime geometry, inertial mass enters physical theory as an assumed property of matter. The equivalence principle establishes empirical equality between inertial and gravitational mass, yet offers no dynamical explanation for its origin. Despite more than a century of theoretical development, inertia remains conceptually external to the geometric structure of gravity.
Historically, Mach proposed that inertia might arise from interaction with the global distribution of matter in the Universe. Although General Relativity partially incorporates relational aspects of motion, it does not fully realize a dynamical mechanism through which cosmological structure determines local

inertial response. Contemporary approaches exploring emergent gravity, vacuum response, and effective field theories have revived the possibility that inertia may originate from interactions with underlying degrees of freedom associated with spacetime itself.
The Cosmic Broth framework advances this perspective by postulating the existence of a universal correlated substrate whose dynamical response generates inertial behavior. Previous work has shown that such a substrate can reproduce universal inertial response as an infrared fixed point, account for gravitational lensing constraints through equilibrium stress–energy contributions, and predict laboratory-scale inertial memory effects arising from finite relaxation dynamics.
However, a central challenge remains. Many emergent frameworks introduce phenomenological scales whose physical origin remains unspecified. In particular, the appearance of a characteristic galactic acceleration scale comparable to cHOcH_OcHO suggests a connection between local dynamics and cosmological structure that standard formulations leave unexplained.
The present work addresses this issue by imposing structural constraints derived from cosmological causality. If the substrate constitutes a physical medium extending across spacetime, its correlation length cannot exceed the cosmological horizon. This requirement fixes the maximal relaxation time of substrate dynamics and leads naturally to a fundamental relation connecting cosmological expansion, inertial response, and emergent acceleration scales.
Within this picture, inertia becomes a cosmological local phenomenon: locally measured resistance to acceleration reflects interaction with a medium whose dynamical memory is bounded by the causal history of the Universe itself.
The purpose of this paper is therefore threefold:
to derive the effective dynamics of matter interacting with a causally constrained substrate,
to establish a fundamental relation linking substrate correlation scales to cosmological expansion,
to demonstrate how gravitational, inertial, and dark-sector phenomena emerge as distinct dynamical regimes of the same underlying medium.
The paper is organized as follows. Section II introduces the dynamical substrate model and causal correlation constraints. Section III derives the effective nonlocal inertial action. Section IV formulates the fundamental substrate relation and emergent acceleration scale. Section V discusses geometric and cosmological implications. Section VI examines observational and experimental consequences. Conclusions follow.

Discussion: Scope, Limitations, and Theoretical Risks

Relation to Modified Gravity and Modified Inertia Frameworks
The Cosmic Broth framework shares conceptual territory with both modified gravity and modified inertia approaches, yet differs structurally from each.
Modified gravity theories typically alter geometric field equations directly, introducing additional fields or nonlinear curvature terms. Modified inertia approaches instead alter the response of matter without modifying spacetime geometry.
The present framework occupies an intermediate position:
spacetime geometry remains governed by Einstein equations,
equilibrium substrate stress–energy contributes to curvature,
inertial response emerges dynamically from substrate interaction.
Thus gravitational and inertial modifications arise from different dynamical limits of a single medium rather than independent theoretical assumptions.
Importantly, the theory does not replace dark matter phenomenology but reinterprets it as equilibrium behavior of underlying degrees of freedom.

Is the Framework Merely a Reparameterization?
A central concern is whether the Cosmic Broth represents only a reformulation of existing phenomenology.
This risk is genuine.
Any effective theory capable of reproducing observed dynamics may appear indistinguishable from parameterized dark-sector models unless it reduces independent scales or produces cross-domain constraints.
The structurally constrained formulation attempts to avoid this degeneracy by linking: a⋆,τΨ,𝘗Ψa_\star,\quad \tau_\Psi,\quad \ell_\Psia⋆,τΨ,𝘗Ψ
to the single cosmological quantity H(t)H(t)H(t).
If this linkage fails observationally, the framework collapses back into phenomenological equivalence with standard models.
Hence the theory derives significance only insofar as the structural constraint survives empirical testing.

/
Global Constraints vs Local Lorentz Symmetry
Another potential objection concerns the introduction of cosmological information into local inertial physics.
The framework does not introduce explicit preferred-frame dynamics at the level of fundamental equations. Instead, global boundary conditions associated with cosmological expansion limit correlation structure of the substrate.
This situation is analogous to thermodynamic systems whose local properties depend on global constraints without violating microscopic symmetries.
Local Lorentz invariance therefore remains intact, while inertial parameters inherit dependence on cosmological background through causal limits rather than symmetry breaking.

Saturation and Large-Scale Behavior
The weak-field closure introduced in Sec. V cannot remain valid arbitrarily far from matter sources. Since the correlation length is horizon-limited, substrate response must saturate or transition near scales comparable to 𝘗Ψ\ell_\Psi𝘗Ψ.
Failure to include such saturation would lead to unphysical exponential growth of effective mass contributions.

The present work therefore defines only the local asymptotic regime r≪𝘗Ψr \ll \ell_\Psir≪𝘗Ψ. A complete nonlinear treatment of substrate equilibration remains an open problem and represents one of the primary theoretical tasks required for future development.

Stability and Nonlocal Dynamics
Nonlocal response theories often suffer from runaway solutions or acausal propagation. In the Cosmic Broth framework, the memory kernel is explicitly retarded and exponentially damped, preventing advanced solutions.
Nevertheless, a fully covariant treatment beyond derivative expansion is necessary to rigorously demonstrate stability in strongly time-dependent gravitational environments.
This limitation should be regarded as methodological rather than conceptual; similar challenges arise in radiation-reaction and open-system formulations across physics.

Parameter Economy and Predictive Power
The central motivation of the structurally constrained approach is parameter reduction.

Standard cosmological modeling introduces independent parameters describing dark matter abundance and dynamical behavior. Here, the emergent acceleration scale follows from cosmological expansion itself:
a⋆∼cH.a_\star \sim cH.a⋆∼cH.
If confirmed observationally, this reduces phenomenological freedom by replacing empirical constants with causal structure.
Conversely, failure of this relation would immediately invalidate the constrained formulation.
